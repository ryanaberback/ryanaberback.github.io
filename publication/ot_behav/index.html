Oxytocin Promotes Positive Relational Behaviour Among Individuals With Major Depressive Disorder During Interpersonal Therapy

Ryan Aberback, Daniela Ioachim, Christopher Cardoso, Lisa Serravalle, Mark A. Ellenbogen
January 2021
PDF

Abstract

Oxytocin (OT) is associated with cooperation, pro-social communication, and empathy in humans. As such, research has investigated OT’s clinical efficacy in treating disorders of social dysfunction, such as major depressive disorder (MDD). However, no studies have examined how OT affects behaviours during psychotherapy. The present study examined how the administration of oxytocin prior to psychotherapy sessions, relative to a placebo, influenced relational behaviour during psychotherapy. Participants (N = 24) diagnosed with MDD were administered 24 international units (6 sprays of a nasal spray bottle) of OT or placebo prior to video-recorded psychotherapy sessions. Using an in house coding scheme, behavioural coders observed 20 minutes of the first two sessions, middle two sessions, and last two psychotherapy sessions of each participant. Behavioural coders were blind to the experimental conditions. Analyses revealed a main effect of drug on positive behaviour (F (1, 22) = 5.41, p = .03, η2p = .20), however there was no main effect of drug on negative behaviour (F (1, 22) = 0.35, p = .56, η2p = .02). Such findings strengthen the view that OT promotes pro-social behaviour, and may be a useful therapeutic tool to help attenuate interpersonal dysfunction among patients with MDD. Additionally, OT may influence behaviours that are indirectly responsible for fostering a strong therapist-client alliance.
